Project Overview

This project explores the design and validation of a low-cost, lightweight ISR fixed-wing UAV incorporating a FPV vision-based click-to-track capability under strict computational, mass, and manufacturability constraints. The primary objective is to demonstrate that stable target tracking and flight control can be achieved using minimal onboard processing and commercial off-the-shelf components, without reliance on high-performance embedded computers.The system integrates a lightweight vision tracker with a custom Kalman filter implemented on an ESP32 companion microcontroller, which smooths and predicts target coordinates prior to injection into the flight controller.

This architecture explicitly addresses vision and communication latency, reducing aggressive control inputs that would otherwise induce excessive ESC thermal loading, actuator wear, and degraded endurance. By prioritising smooth control behaviour, the design enables the use of lighter propulsion hardware and contributes to improved flight efficiency. UAV frame performance was evaluated using XFLR5 followed with manufacturing and material testing, allowing controlled assessment of CG testing with payload placement assessing impacts of vibration and mounting tests. Tracking system performance was evaulated using HITL - Python OPENCV with esp32 to assess latency effects, estimator behaviour, and closed-loop coordinate accuracy response. The results demonstrate that Kalman-based prediction significantly mitigates latency-induced phase lag, improving tracking stability within the operational limits imposed by low-cost telemetry and embedded hardware.

Manufacture Sequence

Material Selection & Preparation: I utilised Aluminum 6061 for the primary housing due to its excellent strength-to-weight ratio and machinability. Initial stock was rough-cut using a Horizontal Bandsaw, leaving a 2mm margin for precision facing.

Manual Milling Operations:: Using a Vertical Milling Machine, I squared the stock to within a 5mm tolerance. Key skills included using a Dial Test Indicator to "tram" the mill head and an Edge Finder to establish a precise (0,0) datum for all subsequent pocketing and drilling operations.

Precision Hole Placement: Based on the Engineering Drawings, I used a Center Drill to prevent bit deflection, followed by a sequence of twist drills and a Reamer to ensure the hinge bores were perfectly concentric and smooth.

Benchwork & Finishing: Final assembly involved manual Hand Tapping for threaded fasteners and extensive Deburring using a swiveling blade tool to ensure all edges were safe and professional.

Technical Skills Demonstrated

Metrology: Proficient use of Vernier Calipers, Micrometers, and Bore Gauges for quality control.

Blueprint Interpretation: Translating complex GD&T (Geometric Dimensioning and Tolerancing) symbols into physical machining steps.

Tooling Selection:Choosing appropriate Carbide vs. HSS (High-Speed Steel) cutters based on the material's surface feet per minute (SFM) requirements.

Engineering Challenge: Mitigating Tool Chatter

One significant challenge during the milling of the main toolbox cavity was Workpiece Vibration (Chatter). As the end mill reached the center of the aluminum block, a high-pitched resonant frequency began to develop, resulting in a poor surface finish and visible "wavy" marks on the interior walls.

Diagnosis and Resolution: I identified that the vibration was caused by an imbalance between the tool's rigidity and the spindle speed. To resolve this, I performed the following adjustments:

Reduced Spindle Speed (RPM):I lowered the RPM to move out of the resonant frequency of the setup.

Adjusted Feed Rate:I increased the Feed per Tooth to ensure the cutter was "biting" into the material rather than rubbing against it, which stabilized the tool pressure.

Increased Rigidity:I re-clamped the workpiece closer to the vise jaws to minimize the "cantilever effect" of the material.
